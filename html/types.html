Pollen Types

Common Pollen Types

Learn about different types of pollen that affect air quality and allergies, and examples of each.

Tree Pollen

Common in spring, tree pollen from oak, birch, and maple trees can trigger seasonal allergies.

Birch Tree

Common in the Northeast, Upper Midwest, and parts of the Atlantic. Abundant in Minnesota, Wisconsin, New York, and New England.

Blooms March to May, peaking in April.

Cedar Tree

Found in the South and Southwest, especially Texas, Oklahoma, and parts of Arizona.

Bloom season: December to March, peak in January-February.

Oak Tree

Abundant in Southeast, Midwest, and Northeast: Texas, Georgia, Missouri, Pennsylvania, and New York.

Blooms March to June, peaking April-May.

Grass

Peak season is late spring to early summer. Bermuda and Timothy grass are major contributors.

Fescue Grass

Found in Midwest, Mid-Atlantic, and parts of Northeast: Ohio, Pennsylvania, New York.

Blooms May to July.

Bahia Grass

Native and widespread in Southeast and Gulf Coast: Florida, Louisiana, Alabama, Georgia.

Bloom April to October.

Rye Grass

Common in Pacific Northwest, Northeast, and northern Midwest: Washington, Oregon, New York, Michigan.

Blooms April to June.

Weed Pollen

Most active in fall; ragweed is the most common weed pollen causing allergic reactions.

Ragweed

Common in Midwest, Northeast, and Southeast: Illinois, Ohio, Pennsylvania, New York, Tennessee.

Blooms August to November, peak in September.

Russian Thistle

Found in arid/semi-arid Southwest: Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, California.

Blooms August to October.

Pigweed

Common in Midwest and Southern states: Arkansas, Oklahoma, Texas, Missouri.

Blooms July to October.
